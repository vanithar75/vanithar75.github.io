In the earlier post , I compared the edge platform vs. the multi-cloud management platform. In this post, I will explain the “Telco Native” MEC platform and compare it against the other options.

The MEC platform is a cloud-like IT service environment available within the telecom network. The Telecom industry is regulated and has regional flavors depending on the regulatory requirements. For example, in Singapore, there is no private spectrum available for commercial enterprise customers. This is quite different from the European/American market, where enterprises can have their own dedicated spectrum for Private Network (5G) deployment. MNOs might have inherent dependencies with the previous generation networks, which affects the consistency (i.e. features might not be supported uniformly across the MNOs within a region). This has to be taken into consideration during the MEC/Edge computing solution design as the regulatory restrictions would be a barrier to replicating the same private network deployment model/Enterprise Edge deployments AS-IS across all the regional facilities.

Some key terminologies are to be understood here.

Non-Public Network - NPN

While there are many possible configurations of NPNs, the below 2 configurations are typically implemented by MNOs for technical (5G features not fully enabled/ Network is not programmable) and regulatory reasons (spectrum availability for enterprises).

In countries where there is no dedicated spectrum available for commercial enterprise customer use, public spectrum shall be used to provide private connectivity. From the central UPF/User Plane, installed in the Telco central DC a DNN (Enterprise DNN/Private DNN) connectivity would be extended to the customer premise.

Telecom Application Function (AF)

An IT-centric definition of an AF would be an Application Server. This link explains the AF terminology in detail.

“The application function was created as a concept several years back as part of 3GPP standardization. In IP media subsystem (IMS) architecture, the traffic generated by services or applications often uses two “planes,” the control plane and the data plane. In this setup, the AF usually sits in the control plane and establishes the quality of service and potentially some charging aspects for a service. The AF acts as a quality controller for specific applications which reside on the network and interconnects with a policy charging and rules function element.”

Generally, in an Internet data world/OTT, the two planes are often not distinguished, as there is only one best-effort data pipe (serving both the traffic). With the increasing need to provide guaranteed services for mission-critical, business critical applications, autonomous platforms and provide real-time Quality of Experience in customer engagements it is required to integrate these AF functions during solution development (Vertical Applications).

UPF - User Plane Function

The UPF bridges the external networks (DNs) to the gNodeB serving the UE by encapsulating the traffic into GTP . With 5G the MEC platform allows users to be connected to the best UPF for their needs, typically one located closer to the user.

The primary purpose is to encapsulate traffic destined from external networks to subscribers into GTP-U packets and forward them onto the gNodeB serving that subscriber, and vice versa.

5G Service-Based Architecture

Per the 5G SBA, the control plane is shared with all the traffic types. However, depending on the state of maturity of the MNO network and the platform capability there shall be some limitations in accessing the common service bus.

Public MEC Deployment Options

The MEC platform is an Application Function (AF) and may interact with the 5G system for the below

· Influence the application traffic routing decisions, e.g., UPF reselection

· NEF for network capabilities exposure

· To interact with the policy framework for policy control

Based on the PLMN management domain of the operator there are two locations where the Public MEC platform can be deployed in an operator network for central office deployment. This depends on the state of maturity of the MNO and use case needs.

Trusted AF - These AFs reside inside the trust domain of the operator, and these are owned and operated by a network operator. These AFs are allowed to have direct communication with 5GC network functions viz., PCF, etc.,

Untrusted AF - These AFs reside outside the trust domain of the network operator and access the 5GC over NEF. An untrusted AF may be owned and operated by an operator or external entities such as a cloud or edge service provider. What kind of information is to be exposed to MEC, as an untrusted AF to better support operations while maintaining the security and privacy of the operator’s network is dependent on the operator network policies.

Trusted DN Vs Untrusted DN

Trusted DN has direct access to 5GC network functions. MNO/Operators typically don’t allow 3rd parties to onboard onto a trusted data network.

Private MEC Platform Deployment Options

The availability of a dedicated spectrum for commercial enterprise customers limits the options for Private MEC deployment to Scenario-3 in the 3GPP NPN definition. The UPF is an MNO/PLMN Operator-managed component. UPF doesn't expose any network context information to the edge application. The exposure layer is independent and is dependent on the network capabilities..

Using a cloud-out solution (Azure stack edge, AWS Outpost, etc.,) provides a local breakout solution for data isolation and doesn't really provide the network context information to the edge application. I would consider this as a traditional edge computing solution to do local data processing. i.e. a local breakout solution from the MNO, and edge applications managed by service provider.

Managing the lifecycle of edge applications through the MEC platform enables and simplifies the integration of the network context information. Edge applications are able to leverage the capabilities that are intrinsic to the Telco MEC platform. Hence the MNO MEC platform has some unique capabilities to enable the next-gen applications requiring high performance.

Is the cloud out solution viz., Azure Stack Edge, AWS Outpost, etc., a MEC platform?

No. These are edge computing solutions. The key capabilities are compute, and storage resource management, and is installed in the N6 interface as an External Data network of the telecom network. These solutions typically use the native public cloud management interfaces and don’t integrate with the MNO MEC orchestrator (central system component from the PLMN Operator). It doesn’t provide the network context information to the devices.

To enable the MEC platform capabilities only if the MNO leverages the network edge solution (Azure for Operators, AWS Wavelength, etc.,) from the public cloud providers, the network capabilities could be exposed through public cloud managed services. This introduces the single-vendor dependency on the network edge and the enterprise edge. i.e.,2 stakeholders (MNO, Enterprise customer) need to be aligned with a single cloud solution.

An alternate integration option could be through the 5GC northbound APIs to provide more network context to the application, but the capabilities depend on the network exposure interface from the MNO. In this approach, the management interface will be the public cloud management interface.

Is the Multi-cloud management platform a MEC platform?

No. The MEC platform could also be a multi-cloud resource manager, but the other way might not be true (this needs to be analyzed further based on the vendor solution capabilities and maturity).

Modern cloud-native edge orchestrators are multi-purpose and can support NF, AF, and cloud resource management and orchestration.

Telco Native MEC platform - Enabler for Service Differentiation

To create differentiating services with the MEC platform, the enabler services i.e. rich context information from the telecom network need to be integrated into the vertical/industry solutions. There are multiple approaches for this and requires a dedicated post to discuss this topic.

Vendor/Partner Solution Capability and MEC Service Agility

This depends very much on the existing infrastructure from the MNO and the 5GC design as explained in the earlier posts [ 1 ],[ 2 ],[ 3 ].

The vendor solutions follow the 2 predominant patterns i.e. cloud-out vs edge-in.

IT platform vendors offering multi-cloud management platforms could add the additional capabilities required for the MEC platform in their solution but could take a longer time to develop the capabilities. Network Equipment Vendors and Telco ISVs could also help to create differentiating enabler services and accelerate the new MEC service development.

Some solutions bring in additional constraints and dependencies to multiple stakeholders e.g. MNO and Enterprise customers need to be the same public cloud offering. It's desirable to have an open architecture with few external dependencies to accelerate the new service development and enable new platform business models.

Each option has its own trade-off and is to be decided based on the context, and business objectives. I hope you gained some information on the similarities and differences between the Telco Native MEC vs. Edge vs. multi-cloud management platforms.
